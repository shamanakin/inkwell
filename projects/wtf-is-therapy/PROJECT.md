## Vision

**What**:
A no-bullshit book about therapy—what it actually is, what it isn't, why people are confused about it, and what they can realistically expect. Written from the perspective of someone who went through therapy as a client, then became a therapist, and now wants to demystify the whole thing.

**Why**:
Therapy is mystified, commodified, and misunderstood. People either think it's magic or a scam. Therapists themselves often perform a role instead of being human. This book gives people permission—permission to be skeptical, permission to ask questions, and (for therapists) permission to drop the halo and be themselves.

**Success Looks Like**:
- Prospective clients finish feeling less confused and more empowered to engage with therapy on their own terms
- Therapists finish feeling seen and liberated from performative professionalism
- The reader exhales and thinks: "Finally, someone said it."

## Audience

**Primary Audience**:
People who are curious about therapy but have no idea what it actually is. They may be considering going, or may have tried and felt confused/disappointed. They're skeptical of hype but open to honest explanation.

**Secondary Audiences**:
- Therapists (especially newer ones) who feel pressure to perform and want permission to be real
- People who have been in therapy and want to make sense of what happened
- Mental health adjacent professionals who want a demystified view

**Reader State**:
Skeptical, curious, possibly a little defensive. May have absorbed cultural ideas about therapy (from TV, social media, friends) that are wildly off-base. Wants straight talk, not jargon.

**What They Already Know**:
- Therapy exists
- It involves talking to someone
- It costs money
- Some people say it helped them, others say it didn't

**What They Need**:
- Demystification: what actually happens in the room
- Permission: to question, to have opinions, to fire their therapist
- Framework: how to evaluate whether therapy is working
- Humanity: to see therapists as people, not gurus

## Constraints

**Ethical Constraints**:
- Don't shit on other therapists (critique systems, not individuals)
- Don't discourage people from getting help when they need it
- Acknowledge this is one perspective, not gospel
- No clinical advice that substitutes for actual therapy
- Trauma-informed: assume readers may carry pain

**Factual Constraints**:
- Ground claims in real clinical experience and research when possible
- Be honest about what's established vs. what's opinion
- Don't promise outcomes
- Acknowledge the limits of the author's perspective

**Tonal Constraints**:
- Direct, slightly irreverent, warm underneath
- Swearing is fine but not gratuitous
- Smart and accessible (no jargon without explanation)
- Permission-giving without being preachy
- Funny where it lands naturally

**Scope Boundaries**:
- This is not a how-to-do-therapy manual
- Not covering every modality in depth
- Not an academic text
- Focus on demystification and permission, not comprehensive guide

**Length/Format Constraints**:
- Book length: TBD (likely 8-12 chapters, moderate length)
- Accessible prose, short chapters, clear structure
- Can be read cover-to-cover or dipped into
